1. GOVERNANCE & STRUCTURE

Understanding our community organization

BMPOA OVERVIEW

The Blue Mountain Property Owners Association (BMPOA) serves as the governing body for our mountain community. As a homeowner in Blue Mountain, you are automatically a member of this association, which is dedicated to preserving and enhancing our unique mountain lifestyle.

PURPOSE & MISSION

As outlined in our bylaws, the purpose of the BMPOA is:

  • To promote the health, safety, and general welfare of its members
  • To preserve the natural beauty of the subdivision and its environment
  • To assist members with matters relating to both internal roads and public roads
  • To oversee community recreation facilities
  • To enforce the Covenants
  • To assist members with federal, state, and local matters affecting their property

LEGAL STATUS

The BMPOA is a legally established non-profit organization incorporated under Virginia law. The Association operates according to its bylaws and the Blue Mountain Subdivision Declaration of Protective Covenants, Conditions & Restrictions, which run with the land and are binding on all property owners.

YOUR RELATIONSHIP WITH BMPOA

As a Blue Mountain property owner, you are automatically a member of the Association. There are no additional dues required for membership, as community maintenance and services are funded through the Blue Mountain Sanitary District tax, which is collected with your Warren County property taxes.

SANITARY DISTRICT EXPLAINED

One of the most important aspects of Blue Mountain's governance is its designation as a Sanitary District. This special status provides significant benefits to our community and is essential to understand as a property owner.

WHAT IS A SANITARY DISTRICT?

A Sanitary District is a special taxing district allowed by the Code of Virginia. It provides a mechanism for communities like ours to fund necessary infrastructure and services through a dedicated tax assessment on properties within the district.

Key Benefit

Our Sanitary District status ensures that all property owners, including developers, contribute fairly to the maintenance of our roads and community assets.

BLUE MOUNTAIN SANITARY DISTRICT

The Blue Mountain Subdivision Sanitary District was created by Order of the Circuit Court of Warren County. By agreement with the Warren County Board of Supervisors, the BMPOA Executive Board serves as the managing agent of the Sanitary District.

KEY BENEFITS OF SANITARY DISTRICT STATUS

Our Sanitary District status provides numerous advantages:

  • Uniform Funding: Establishes consistent fees throughout the entire subdivision
  • Reliable Collection: Taxes are collected by the Warren County Treasurer's Office, ensuring a high collection rate
  • Tax Deductibility: Sanitary district taxes may be deductible on federal income tax returns (consult your tax advisor)
  • Universal Participation: All lots, including those owned by developers, are subject to the tax
  • Property Value Protection: Unpaid taxes must be satisfied before property sales, protecting community resources
  • Disaster Relief Eligibility: Qualifies the community for various state and federal disaster relief funds
  • Private Road Protection: Sanitary district status does not affect the privacy of our roads or other amenities

BOARD & COMMITTEES STRUCTURE

The BMPOA is managed by an elected Board of Officers and Directors who volunteer their time to serve the community. The Board consists of:

  • Five Officers (President, First Vice President, Second Vice President, Secretary, and Treasurer)
  • Four Directors-at-Large

All Board members are Blue Mountain property owners elected at the Annual Meeting for two-year terms. The Board operates with several standing committees that focus on specific areas of community management:

  • Roads Committee: Oversees maintenance and improvement of our private road network
  • Architectural Review Committee (ARC): Reviews and approves all construction and exterior modifications
  • Recreation Committee: Manages community recreational facilities and events
  • Communications Committee: Handles newsletters, website, and other community communications

MEMBERSHIP & MEETINGS

All property owners within the Blue Mountain Subdivision are automatically members of the BMPOA. Membership provides you with voting rights and the ability to participate in community decision-making.

Annual Meeting

The BMPOA holds an Annual Meeting, typically in May, where members elect Board members, review the annual budget, and address important community matters. All property owners are encouraged to attend.

Board Meetings

The Board of Directors meets regularly throughout the year. These meetings are open to all members. Meeting dates and times are posted on the website and in community newsletters.

Special Meetings

Occasionally, special meetings may be called to address urgent or significant matters. Notice of these meetings will be provided in accordance with the bylaws.

GET INVOLVED

Your participation strengthens our community! Consider attending meetings, joining a committee, or running for a Board position. Contact any Board member to learn about current volunteer opportunities.

2. FIRE SAFETY & EMERGENCY PREPAREDNESS

Critical information to keep your family and property safe

WILDFIRE RISK UNDERSTANDING

Blue Mountain's wooded environment, while beautiful, presents significant wildfire risks that all community members should be aware of. Our location in a Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) area means homes are interspersed with forests, increasing vulnerability to wildfires.

FIRE SEASON

  • Spring Fire Season: February 15 - April 30
  • Fall Fire Season: October 15 - November 30
  • Summer drought conditions can extend risk periods

BURN BAN INFORMATION

During designated fire seasons, Virginia law restricts open burning before 4:00 PM. All fires must be attended at all times and completely extinguished before leaving. Check with Warren County Fire & Rescue for current burn restrictions before burning anything outdoors.

UNDERSTANDING RISK LEVELS

High Risk Areas
Steep slopes, areas with dense vegetation, properties with unmaintained defensible space, and homes with wooden decks/roofs are at highest risk.

Moderate Risk Areas
Properties with partial defensible space, some vegetation management, and homes with some fire-resistant features but remaining vulnerabilities.

Lower Risk Areas
Properties with well-maintained defensible space (at least 30ft), fire-resistant landscaping, and homes with fire-resistant roofing and construction.

COMMUNITY FIRE PREVENTION EFFORTS

The BMPOA works with local fire authorities and the Virginia Department of Forestry to implement fire prevention measures:

  • Annual wood-chipping program to reduce fuel loads
  • Community-wide evacuation planning
  • Educational programs on defensible space
  • Participation in the Firewise USA program

  • If you see a wildfire, call 911 immediately

HOME FIRE PROTECTION STRATEGIES

Creating a defensible space around your home is essential to reducing wildfire risk. These strategies can significantly increase your home's chances of surviving a wildfire.

DEFENSIBLE SPACE ZONES

Zone 1: 0-30 feet from structures

  • Remove all flammable materials including dead vegetation, woodpiles, and propane tanks
  • Keep grass mowed and irrigated if possible
  • Prune tree branches up 6-10 feet from the ground
  • Space trees so crowns are at least 10 feet apart
  • Choose fire-resistant plants for landscaping
  • Keep gutters and roof clear of leaves and debris
  • Move patio furniture, doormats, and decorative items away during high fire danger

Zone 2: 30-100 feet from structures

  • Reduce and space vegetation
  • Remove ladder fuels (vegetation that allows fire to climb from ground into tree canopy)
  • Create fuel breaks with driveways, walkways, and lawns
  • Keep firewood stacks at least 30 feet from structures

HOME HARDENING MEASURES

Roof & Gutters:

  • Install Class A fire-rated roofing materials (metal recommended)
  • Use non-combustible gutter covers
  • Regularly clean gutters and roof of debris

Vents & Openings:

  • Cover all vents with 1/8-inch metal mesh
  • Box in eaves with non-combustible materials
  • Install weather stripping around garage doors

WOOD-CHIPPING PROGRAM

BMPOA offers an annual wood-chipping program to help residents reduce fuel loads and improve defensible space. This is an excellent opportunity to clear branches and brush from your property to maintain defensible space.

FAMILY EMERGENCY PLANNING

Creating a family emergency plan specifically for wildfire events is essential for all Blue Mountain residents. This preparation can make all the difference during an evacuation situation.

DEVELOP YOUR EVACUATION PLAN

Your family's evacuation plan should be:

  • Simple enough to be followed when under stress
  • Detailed enough to cover key elements
  • Understood by everyone residing in your home
  • Practiced regularly to ensure familiarity
  • Updated as your family situation changes

FAMILY EVACUATION PLAN CHECKLIST

Identify two escape routes from each room
Choose multiple evacuation routes from your neighborhood
Establish meeting places: near your home and outside the area
Designate an out-of-area contact person
Plan for pets and livestock evacuation
Prepare an emergency supply kit
Create a home inventory with photos for insurance
Identify special needs (elderly, disabled, children)

EVACUATION ALERT LEVELS

Level 1 – READY: Be aware of danger in your area. Monitor emergency services websites and local media. Pack essential items and prepare to leave if necessary.

Level 2 – SET: There is significant danger in your area. Be prepared to leave at a moment's notice. Consider voluntarily relocating to a safe area.

Level 3 – GO: Imminent danger exists. Leave immediately. Delay puts your life at risk. Do not return until officials declare the area safe.

STAY INFORMED

Sign up for emergency notifications through Smart911.com and monitor local radio stations WZRV 95.3 FM and WFTR 1450 AM during emergencies.

3. COMMUNITY SERVICES & AMENITIES

Features and services available to residents

ROADS & MAINTENANCE

The private roads within Blue Mountain are maintained by the BMPOA through Sanitary District taxes. Our Roads Committee oversees maintenance, snow removal, and improvements to our extensive road network. Key points:

  • Regular grading and maintenance occurs throughout the year
  • Snow removal is prioritized by road usage and steepness
  • Culverts and drainage systems are maintained to prevent washouts
  • Major repairs and improvements are scheduled based on available funding

REPORT ROAD ISSUES

If you notice a road hazard or damage that needs attention, please contact the Roads Committee Chair. For emergency road issues that affect access or safety, call 911.

RECREATION AREAS

Blue Mountain offers several recreational facilities for residents to enjoy:

  • The Lodge: Community building available for resident use and rental located at 540 Cliff Road. Great for gatherings and events.
  • Deer Lake: Small lake open for fishing, non-motorized boating, and swimming during warmer months.
  • Hiking Trails: Several designated hiking trails offer opportunities to enjoy our natural surroundings.

REFUSE COLLECTION & DISPOSAL

Garbage and recycling services are available through private vendors in our area. The BMPOA does not provide refuse collection. Residents are responsible for contracting their own service or taking trash to the Warren County Transfer Station.

Please note these important guidelines:

  • Store trash in bear-resistant containers to prevent wildlife conflicts
  • Recycle according to Warren County guidelines
  • Large item disposal is available at the county transfer station
  • Burning of trash is prohibited by county ordinance

4. SEASONAL CONSIDERATIONS

Be prepared for each season on Blue Mountain

SPRING (March-May)

  • Participate in wood-chipping program
  • Clear culverts and drainage of winter debris
  • Watch for bears emerging from hibernation
  • Begin maintaining defensible space
  • Check for road damage after winter
  • Inspect roof, gutters, and foundation after winter
  • Test outdoor water systems

SUMMER (June-August)

  • Maintain defensible space as vegetation grows
  • Be vigilant with fire safety during dry periods
  • Secure all food sources to avoid bear encounters
  • Watch for summer thunderstorms and lightning
  • Enjoy community activities and recreation areas
  • Keep emergency water supply available
  • Watch for ticks and other pests

FALL (September-November)

  • Clean gutters of falling leaves regularly
  • Prepare your home for winter
  • Stock emergency supplies
  • Watch for increased deer activity during rut season
  • Follow fall fire season burning restrictions
  • Winterize outdoor water systems
  • Ensure heating systems are serviced

WINTER (December-February)

  • Keep an emergency kit in your vehicle
  • Have alternate heating sources available
  • Know snow removal priorities and plan accordingly
  • Watch for ice on roads, especially in shaded areas
  • Check on elderly neighbors during storms
  • Keep adequate food and water supplies
  • Have backup power options available

WINTER DRIVING PREPARATION

Mountain roads can be particularly challenging during winter. Always:

  • Maintain a 4-wheel or all-wheel drive vehicle if possible
  • Keep tire chains or cables in your vehicle
  • Have a full tank of gas during winter weather events
  • Pack emergency supplies (blankets, water, food, flashlight)
  • Avoid unnecessary travel during winter storms
