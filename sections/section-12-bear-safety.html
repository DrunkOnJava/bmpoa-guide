BEAR SAFETY

Living in harmony with black bears requires understanding their behavior and taking proactive steps to prevent conflicts. This section provides essential information for keeping your family, property, and our bear population safe.

UNDERSTANDING BEAR BEHAVIOR

Black bears are intelligent and typically avoid humans. Most conflicts occur because bears seek out food attractants, not out of aggression. Understanding bear behavior helps us coexist peacefully with these magnificent animals that share our mountain home.

BLACK BEAR FACTS

  • Virginia's black bear population has grown significantly in recent decades
  • Bears have excellent memories and will return to reliable food sources
  • They can smell food from over a mile away
  • Bears are most active in spring and fall when natural food is scarce
  • A fed bear becomes a problem bear - and often a dead bear
  • Adult males weigh 200-500 pounds; females 100-300 pounds
  • Despite their size, bears can run 35 mph and climb trees easily
  • Bears are omnivores with ~80% plant-based diet
  • They have color vision and detect movement well
  • Bears communicate via body language and various sounds

BEAR BEHAVIOR MYTHS DEBUNKED

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS

  • Myth: Bears standing on hind legs are aggressive
    Truth: They're trying to see or smell better
  • Myth: Mother bears always attack to defend cubs
    Truth: Mothers usually flee or send cubs up trees when threatened
  • Myth: Relocating bears solves problems
    Truth: Another bear often moves into the vacant territory

UNDERSTANDING BEAR COMMUNICATION

Visual Cues:

  • Ears forward = curious
  • Ears back = stressed or defensive
  • Huffing or jaw popping = nervous
  • Blowing or snorting = startled

Behavioral Signs:

  • False charging = bluff (usually stops short)
  • Standing up = trying to identify you
  • Circling = assessing situation
  • Tree climbing = escape behavior

COMMON BEAR ATTRACTANTS & PREVENTION

COMMON BEAR ATTRACTANTS

Bears are opportunistic feeders attracted to easy calories. The following items commonly draw bears to residential properties:

  • Bird feeders (April‚ÄìNovember)
  • Unsecured garbage cans left outdoors
  • Pet food on porches or yards
  • Grills with residual grease or scraps
  • Fruit trees (ripe or fallen fruit)
  • Compost piles containing meat or fatty scraps
  • Coolers and trash on porches/decks
  • Beehives without electric fencing
  • Livestock feed and chicken coops
  • Scented items (candles, air fresheners)

PREVENTIVE ACTIONS FOR HOMEOWNERS

BEAR-PROOFING CHECKLIST

Remove bird feeders during active season or switch to bear-resistant models
Store garbage in locked garage, shed, or bear-proof container
Never leave trash outdoors overnight
Feed pets indoors and clean up any food scraps immediately
Clean grills after each use (burn off grease for 5 minutes, then cover)
Use electric fencing around beehives, compost piles, and gardens
Harvest ripe fruit promptly; remove fallen fruit daily
Do not store any food or scented items on porches/decks
Compost responsibly‚Äîno meat, bones, or fatty scraps

BEAR-RESISTANT SOLUTIONS

RECOMMENDED PRODUCTS

  • Bear-proof garbage containers: Heavy-duty with locking lids
  • Electric fencing: Minimum 5 strands, 8,000+ volts
  • Bear-resistant bird feeders: Weight-activated closing mechanisms
  • Motion-activated devices: Lights, sprinklers, or noise makers
  • Bear spray: EPA-approved deterrent for emergency use

COMMUNITY COOPERATION

Bear management requires community-wide effort. One attractant can draw bears to an entire neighborhood. Work with your neighbors to:

  • Coordinate trash storage and collection practices
  • Share information about bear sightings
  • Remove attractants simultaneously
  • Support neighbors in bear-proofing efforts
  • Report persistent problems to authorities

LEGAL REMINDER

Feeding bears (intentionally or not) is illegal under Virginia Code. Placing food, minerals, or trash to attract bears violates state law. Violators face fines and potential legal action. More importantly, fed bears often must be destroyed for public safety.

BEAR ENCOUNTERS & HAZING

IF YOU ENCOUNTER A BEAR

IN YOUR YARD OR HOME

  • Remain calm - do not run (running can trigger chase response)
  • Identify an exit route - back away slowly while making noise
  • Make yourself appear large - raise arms, open jacket
  • Make noise - clap, yell "Go away bear!" in firm voice
  • If indoors - open doors to allow bear to exit; do not trap it
  • Never approach - give the bear space to leave

WHILE HIKING

  • Make noise - talk loudly, clap hands around blind corners
  • Hike in groups - bears avoid larger groups
  • Keep children close and dogs leashed
  • If surprised at close range - speak calmly, back away slowly
  • Avoid direct eye contact - this can be seen as a threat
  • If a bear charges - use bear spray if available
  • If contact is imminent - DO NOT play dead; fight back using any available objects

HAZING & DETERRENTS

Hazing teaches bears to avoid human spaces. It's most effective when done immediately and consistently by the entire community.

SAFE HAZING METHODS

  • Paintballs aimed at bear's rump
  • Air horns or boat horns
  • Shouting and banging pots/pans
  • Motion-activated alarms
  • Water hose (if available)
  • Throwing small objects (not at head)

HAZING SAFETY

  • Always maintain escape route
  • Never corner a bear
  • Don't haze bears with cubs
  • Haze from safe distance/indoors
  • Stop if bear approaches
  • Document incidents for DWR

WHEN TO CALL FOR HELP

Call 911 immediately if:

  • Bear shows aggressive behavior
  • Bear attempts to enter occupied building
  • Bear threatens people or pets
  • Bear appears sick or injured

Call DWR Wildlife Helpline if:

  • Bear repeatedly visits despite hazing
  • Bear causes property damage
  • You need prevention advice
  • Bear has ear tags (research bear)

LIVING WITH BEARS

PROPERTY MODIFICATIONS

Making your property less attractive to bears is a long-term investment in safety and coexistence:

  • Replace fruit trees with non-bearing ornamentals
  • Install motion lights around potential food sources
  • Build secure enclosures for trash and recycling
  • Create clear sight lines by trimming dense shrubs
  • Remove bird baths during drought (bears seek water)
  • Secure crawl spaces under decks and sheds
  • Install electric mats at doorways if needed
  • Consider bear-proof fencing for high-value areas

EDUCATING FAMILY & GUESTS

TEACHING CHILDREN

  • Bears are wild animals, not pets or cartoon characters
  • Never run from a bear - back away slowly
  • Make noise when playing outside
  • Come inside immediately if a bear is spotted
  • Never feed or approach any wildlife

INFORMING GUESTS & RENTERS

Property owners who rent should provide bear safety information to all guests, including:

  • Proper trash storage procedures
  • What to do if they see a bear
  • Emergency contact numbers
  • Location of bear spray (if provided)

BEAR-WISE COMMUNITY PRACTICES

NEIGHBORHOOD COORDINATION

Organize community bear awareness meetings
Create phone/email tree for bear sighting alerts
Coordinate removal of attractants
Share costs for bear-proof infrastructure
Support neighbors dealing with bear issues
Work together on consistent hazing
